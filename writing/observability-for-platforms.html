Observability

Observability foundations for platform teams

By Akkireddy Challa / Updated 2026

Observability is not the number of dashboards a team has. It is the ability to understand what a system is doing, why it is behaving that way, and who should act when the signal matters.

Begin with service ownership

Every useful alert needs an owner. If nobody knows who should respond, the alert becomes background noise. Platform teams can provide standards, but service teams need clear responsibility for their own runtime health.

Choose signals before dashboards

Dashboards are useful after the important signals are known. I start with latency, traffic, errors, saturation, deployment health, dependency health, and cost-sensitive resource usage. Then I design views around questions engineers actually ask during incidents.

Make alerts actionable

An alert should point toward an action. It should include context, severity, service ownership, and a first response path. If an alert only says something changed, it may be better as a dashboard signal than a page.

Connect incidents to platform improvements

The best observability work happens after incidents. Every incident can reveal missing signals, weak runbooks, unclear ownership, or fragile architecture. Platform teams should turn those findings into reusable improvements.

Use tools as part of a system

Datadog, Prometheus, Grafana, CloudWatch, and Google Cloud Monitoring are all useful, but tooling alone is not the foundation. The foundation is a shared model for telemetry, response, and continuous improvement.
